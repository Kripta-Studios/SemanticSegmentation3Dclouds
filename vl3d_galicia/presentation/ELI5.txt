ELI5: 3D LiDAR Semantic Segmentation and Forest Intelligence MVP
================================================================

What this project is
--------------------

Imagine an aircraft flying over a landscape with a laser scanner.

The scanner sends millions of laser pulses toward the ground. Every returned pulse becomes a tiny 3D point. Together, those points form a detailed three-dimensional copy of the territory.

The raw points do not automatically say what they represent. This project teaches a computer to classify them as:

- ground
- low vegetation
- medium vegetation
- high vegetation or trees
- buildings
- water

The simplest explanation is:

    Airborne LiDAR + color + infrared + intensity
                         |
                         v
              Clean 3D point blocks
                         |
                         v
       Local shape, height and density context
                         |
                         v
          Point-by-point AI classification
                         |
                         v
        GIS maps, forest indicators and reports

It is like giving the computer a giant 3D coloring book and asking it to color every point correctly.

What problem it solves
----------------------

LiDAR contains a great deal of useful information, but a territory may contain hundreds of millions of points. A person cannot review and classify every point manually.

Without automation, organizations face:

- slow map production
- expensive manual GIS work
- inconsistent classification
- confusion between roofs and tree crowns
- difficulty separating low and medium vegetation
- too many field visits
- valuable LiDAR data that is never fully used

The product creates an automatic first map so specialists can focus on validation and important areas instead of beginning from an empty GIS layer.

How the model decides what a point is
-------------------------------------

A single point can be ambiguous.

A point ten meters above the ground could be part of a tree, a roof, a tower or a measurement error. The model therefore looks at the point and its surroundings.

For each point, it asks questions such as:

- What exists within 2.5 meters?
- What exists within 5 meters?
- What exists within 10 meters?
- Is the neighborhood flat, curved or irregular?
- Is the area dense or sparse?
- How high is the point relative to nearby ground?
- Do RGB, infrared and laser-intensity values resemble vegetation, water or a roof?

Example:

    A high point
        |
        +-- irregular high neighborhood -> probably a tree crown
        |
        +-- flat rectangular neighborhood -> probably a roof
        |
        +-- isolated from nearby surfaces -> possible noise or unusual object

The extra local geometric features are calculated without using class labels. The final segmentation model is then trained with labeled examples.

What is currently proven
------------------------

The current production candidate is not a fully self-supervised Geo-JEPA model.

The strongest deployable route is:

- supervised point-wise segmentation
- label-free local geometric context
- Taubin-Weingarten-inspired surface features
- robust coordinate and spectral normalization
- focal loss
- class-balanced training

Geo-JEPA and TW-JEPA are implemented research tracks. Frozen JEPA encoders did not beat the strongest supervised models. DINOv2 fusion was also tested, but it did not improve the strong LiDAR baseline.

Two useful model references
---------------------------

There are two important models because "best" depends on the objective.

1. Best internal macro-F1 and mean IoU

    Overall accuracy: 86.49%
    Macro-F1:         82.60%
    Mean IoU:         72.63%

This model is the highest internal mIoU result in the current comparison.

2. Best deployment and geographic-generalization candidate

    Overall accuracy: 86.71%
    Macro-F1:         82.48%
    Mean IoU:         72.50%

This model uses robust normalization and 20,000 class-balanced training blocks. It keeps almost the same internal performance while performing much better on separate geographic data.

Internal class performance of the robust model
----------------------------------------------

    Ground IoU:             76.49%
    Low vegetation IoU:     43.91%
    Medium vegetation IoU:  52.71%
    High vegetation IoU:    94.53%
    Building IoU:           69.51%
    Water IoU:              97.86%

Low vegetation remains the most difficult internal class. High vegetation and water are very strong on the internal distribution.

Has it been tested outside the original region?
------------------------------------------------

Yes.

The model was evaluated on airborne LiDAR tiles from a geographically separate campaign.

External development benchmark
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

On a 32-tile external benchmark, the robust model achieved:

    Overall accuracy: 80.83%
    Macro-F1:         73.42%
    Mean IoU:         60.41%

Compared with a strong TW baseline, it improved:

    Overall accuracy: +8.23 percentage points
    Macro-F1:         +9.43 percentage points
    Mean IoU:         +10.96 percentage points

This first external set was later used to understand domain shift and design the robust-normalization strategy, so it is best described as an external development benchmark.

Fresh external holdout
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

A second set of 32 tiles was selected with no overlap with the first external set.

On this fresh holdout:

    Strong baseline:
        Overall accuracy: 77.33%
        Macro-F1:         51.38%
        Mean IoU:         40.62%

    Robust model:
        Overall accuracy: 83.27%
        Macro-F1:         61.80%
        Mean IoU:         50.98%

Improvement:

    Overall accuracy: +5.94 percentage points
    Macro-F1:         +10.42 percentage points
    Mean IoU:         +10.35 percentage points

The robust model improved all six class IoUs compared with the strong baseline.

Important detail about water and buildings
------------------------------------------

The fresh external holdout contains very little water and few building points:

- building: 0.80% of reliable points
- water: 0.064% of reliable points

This means the comparison against the baseline is useful, but the absolute external water result is not yet representative enough for a strong customer promise. A future external dataset or customer pilot should contain meaningful water and building coverage.

Why the robust model generalizes better
---------------------------------------

The original model learned some statistics that were specific to the original region and sensor campaign.

The external data had different:

- infrared values
- terrain height ranges
- point densities
- class proportions

The solution was not simply to make the neural network larger. The most effective combination was:

1. robust coordinate normalization
2. robust spectral normalization
3. label-free local geometric context
4. true class-balanced selection over 20,000 training blocks

This preserved strong internal performance and improved external geographic generalization.

Forest intelligence layer
-------------------------

The forest-facing layer converts LiDAR classification into practical indicators:

- vegetation ratio
- high-vegetation ratio
- forest-core ratio
- canopy-cover proxy
- canopy-height proxy
- canopy-gap flags
- rule-based anomaly candidates

These are useful operational proxies. They are not certified biomass estimates, species classifications or verified temporal-change measurements.

What Geo-JEPA means here
------------------------

Geo-JEPA is the research direction for learning geographic structure without requiring labels for every example.

The idea is:

    Show most of a 3D landscape block
                  |
                  v
       Hide one spatial region
                  |
                  v
    Predict the hidden representation
                  |
                  v
    Learn reusable geographic structure

This route is implemented and useful for continued research, especially for domain adaptation and future pretraining. It is not currently the strongest production model.

How it is deployed
------------------

Phase 1 - Historical pilot

The customer provides existing LiDAR, the target area and any available reference maps. The system processes a representative sample and produces maps, metrics and forest indicators.

Phase 2 - Validation

The customer's GIS or field team reviews representative zones. Success is measured through accuracy, correction effort, processing time and operational usefulness.

Phase 3 - Full-area processing

The pipeline processes all target tiles and merges the outputs into standard GIS deliverables.

Phase 4 - Integration

Outputs can be delivered to QGIS, ArcGIS, PostGIS, GeoServer, web viewers or customer APIs.

Phase 5 - Repeated monitoring

When aligned surveys from several dates become available, a future extension can compare periods and flag changes.
